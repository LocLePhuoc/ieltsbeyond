Japan had been at the top of my travel list for years. The food, the culture, the landscapes — everything about it fascinated me from a distance. After two weeks on the ground, I can confirm: it lives up to the hype and then some.

## Getting Around

The **Japan Rail Pass** is non-negotiable for a multi-city trip. We used the 14-day pass and it paid for itself within the first three days. The shinkansen (bullet train) is an experience in itself — silent, punctual to the second, and fast enough to make domestic flights feel pointless.

Within cities, get a **Suica or Pasmo card**. Tap in, tap out, never think about buying individual tickets. These cards also work at convenience stores and vending machines, which is more useful than you'd expect.

## Tokyo: Controlled Chaos

Tokyo is overwhelming in the best possible way. Every neighborhood has its own personality. Shibuya is electric, Shinjuku is vast, Akihabara is surreal, and Yanaka feels like stepping back fifty years.

My advice: resist the urge to plan every hour. The best moments came from wandering — stumbling into a tiny ramen shop with six seats, finding a hidden shrine tucked between skyscrapers, watching the sunset from a random rooftop.

## Kyoto: The Quiet Counterpoint

If Tokyo is the future, Kyoto is the past. The bamboo grove in Arashiyama is as magical as the photos suggest, but the real treasures are the smaller temples that don't make it onto the tourist trail. **Tofuku-ji** in autumn and **Nanzen-ji**'s aqueduct were highlights for me.

Stay in a **ryokan** (traditional inn) for at least one night. Sleeping on a futon, bathing in an onsen, and eating a multi-course kaiseki dinner is the kind of experience that sticks with you.

## The Food

I could write an entire post just about the food — and probably will. For now, the essentials: eat at convenience stores (seriously, the onigiri and egg sandwiches are incredible), try a conveyor belt sushi place at least once, and never walk past a yaki-imo cart without buying a roasted sweet potato.

Japan changed how I think about travel. It's a place where ancient and modern exist in genuine harmony, where hospitality is an art form, and where every meal feels like a small revelation. I'm already planning the return trip.
