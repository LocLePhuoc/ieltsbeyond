Everyone knows about Lisbon's trams and Porto's port wine cellars. And they're wonderful — don't skip them. But Portugal has so much more to offer once you step off the well-worn tourist path.

## The Alentejo Region

South of Lisbon and east of the Algarve lies the Alentejo, a vast expanse of rolling plains, cork oak forests, and medieval hilltop towns. It's the kind of landscape that makes you pull the car over every ten minutes just to stare.

**Monsaraz** is the standout. This tiny walled village sits on a hilltop overlooking the Alqueva reservoir — the largest artificial lake in Europe. At sunset, the white walls glow gold against the deep blue water. We had the entire rampart walk to ourselves.

The food here is hearty and unpretentious. Think slow-braised pork cheeks, bread-thickened soups, and wines from estates where the family still handles every harvest. A full lunch with wine rarely costs more than fifteen euros per person.

## Tavira

The Algarve's eastern end is a world away from the resort-packed western coast. Tavira is a small riverside town with Roman bridges, Moorish architecture, and access to some of the quietest beaches in southern Europe.

Take the ferry to **Ilha de Tavira** — a barrier island with miles of empty sand backed by dunes and pine forests. We spent an afternoon there in August and could count the other visitors on two hands.

## The Douro Valley

Most visitors see Porto and move on, missing the Douro Valley just an hour inland. The terraced vineyards along the river are a UNESCO World Heritage site, and for good reason. It's one of the most beautiful cultivated landscapes I've ever seen.

A boat cruise up the river is the classic way to experience it, but I preferred driving the winding roads above. Every bend reveals a new composition of river, vine, and stone.

## Why It Works

Portugal succeeds as a travel destination because it hasn't lost itself to tourism. The smaller towns remain genuinely Portuguese — the cafes serve local regulars, the food reflects regional traditions, and the pace of life moves at a speed that reminds you why you traveled in the first place.
